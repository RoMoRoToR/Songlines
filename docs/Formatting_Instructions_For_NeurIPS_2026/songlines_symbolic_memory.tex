\begin{abstract}
Success-only metrics collapse qualitatively different memory-based
navigation failures into a single scalar, hiding which stage of the
retrieval pipeline is responsible.

The Q/R/M/C decomposition --- query formation, retrieval
satisfaction, target materialization, completion after retrieval ---
is proposed and proved identifiable from trajectory logs under a
conditional stage-Markov assumption (Theorem~1).

\textbf{Single-agent.} On a 10-seed MiniGrid benchmark the framework
localizes bottlenecks: hazard recovery is retrieval-limited (oracle
retrieval lifts success $0.39 \to 0.60$) while goal-region rejoin is
downstream-limited (oracle retrieval rescues semantic-path completion
without moving end-to-end success).

\textbf{Multi-agent.} On a 35{,}640-run sweep with broadcast cadence
$K \in \{1,2,4,8,16,32,48,64\}$ and 40 seeds, the bottleneck-shift
signature appears as a strongly negative within-configuration
correlation between $P(M^\star|R^\star)$ and $P(C^\star|M^\star)$
(Pearson $-0.61$ at $K\!=\!4$, decaying to $-0.02$ at $K\!=\!64$),
and mean time-to-success has an interior minimum at $K\!=\!8$.

\textbf{Honest limit.} The strict interior-maximum corollary on the
conditional product $P(M^\star|R^\star) \cdot P(C^\star|M^\star)$ does
\emph{not} hold --- the product monotonically approaches the
independent-agent boundary. This is reported as a feature of the
framework, not a bug: the slope-level claim of Proposition~2 is
supported while one of its corollaries is not.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Classical navigation benchmarks often assume that the agent is given an explicit destination coordinate or a dense task reward. Many practical navigation problems are different: the agent must reach a place of a certain \emph{type} --- a safe rest area, a water-like resource, a post-hazard recovery exit, or a goal-associated region --- specified by a semantic pattern rather than a fixed $(x, y)$ waypoint. This setting opens a methodological question: can we measure \emph{where} memory-based semantic navigation breaks down, not only \emph{whether} it succeeds?

We propose a four-stage diagnostic framework that answers this question for both single agents and multi-agent collectives. The decomposition is Q/R/M/C: \textbf{Q}uery formation, \textbf{R}etrieval satisfaction, target \textbf{M}aterialization, and \textbf{C}ompletion after retrieval. The decomposition factorizes per-episode success and, under a conditional stage-Markov property, is identifiable from trajectory logs (Theorem~1).

\textbf{Single-agent diagnostics.} Success-only metrics collapse qualitatively different failures into a single scalar. In a 10-seed MiniGrid benchmark, water and rest tasks are strong end-to-end (0.95, 0.93 success), but goal-region rejoin and hazard recovery are substantially harder. The four-stage view turns this from a uniform weakness into actionable attribution: hazard recovery is retrieval-limited (oracle retrieval lifts success $0.39 \to 0.60$), while goal-region rejoin is downstream-limited (oracle retrieval rescues semantic-path completion without changing end-to-end success). The framework therefore produces stage-specific prescriptions instead of undifferentiated evaluations.

\textbf{Multi-agent extension.} When $N$ agents share memory through a
communication protocol with broadcast cadence $K$, the single-agent
factorization extends with enlarged conditioning sets
(Proposition~1$^\star$). The cadence $K$ becomes a structural axis that
the framework allows investigation of diagnostically rather than
end-to-end.

\textbf{Bottleneck shift between M and C stages.} A slope-level claim
(Proposition~2) is stated under stylized assumptions on freshness and
occupancy: as $K$ decreases, $P(M^\star|R^\star)$ rises (fresher peer
memory enables reliable commitment) and $P(C^\star|M^\star)$ falls
(peers race for the same targets). Both slope signs are confirmed on
the 35{,}640-run sweep ($p < 10^{-4}$).

\textbf{What the data do and do not support.} A naive reading of the
two slopes predicts an interior maximum on the conditional product
$P(M^\star|R^\star) \cdot P(C^\star|M^\star)$. In this environment that
prediction fails: the product is monotone in $K$ and approaches the
independent-agent boundary. The interior optimum the framework
intuitively predicts appears instead on mean time-to-success, with a
clean minimum at $K\!=\!8$. The gap is reported explicitly --- it is
the difference between a stage-level operationalisation that can be
\emph{wrong} on specific summaries and a stage-agnostic guess that is
unfalsifiable.

\textbf{Why this framing is timely.} Embodied and goal-conditioned agents increasingly retrieve places, objects, or tools by meaning \citep{andrychowicz2017hindsight,ghosh2021learning,savva2019habitat}. LLM-agent systems lean on explicit memory layers but evaluate mostly end-to-end \citep{wang2023voyager,shinn2023reflexion,packer2023memgpt}. Multi-agent reinforcement learning likewise increasingly involves communicating populations \citep{lowe2017maddpg,foerster2018ctde}, but the question ``which agent should know what, and when?'' lacks a unified diagnostic vocabulary. Our framework supplies one.

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=20pt,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Theorem.} A stage-decomposition framework with a
probabilistic factorization and an identifiability result for single
agents (Theorem~1) and its multi-agent extension (Proposition~1$^\star$).

\item \textbf{Slope-level claim.} A bottleneck-shift claim
(Proposition~2) about how cadence $K$ trades off the M and C stages,
verified empirically ($p < 10^{-4}$ on both slopes).

\item \textbf{System.} A symbolic-semantic memory stack instantiating
the framework, with four communication architectures (independent,
shared-bus, central-aggregator, peer-to-peer broadcast).

\item \textbf{Benchmarks.} (a) 10-seed single-agent MiniGrid benchmark
with oracle stage interventions; (b) 35{,}640-run multi-agent sweep at
$N\!\in\!\{3,5,8\}$ plus 8{,}640-run scale-up at $N\!=\!12$.

\item \textbf{Honest accounting.} Explicit report of what fails: the
strict interior-maximum corollary on the conditional product does not
hold in this environment; the interior optimum reappears on the
efficiency metric.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related work}

Our work sits at the intersection of four areas: semantic navigation, memory-based RL, multi-agent communication, and diagnostic evaluation.

\textbf{Semantic navigation and memory.} Classical cognitive-map and semantic-map perspectives motivate explicit place abstractions \citep{tolman1948cognitive,okeefe1978hippocampus,kuipers2000spatial}. Modern embodied AI combines mapping and planning \citep{gupta2017cognitive} or exposes explicit topological structure (SPTM, \citealp{savinov2018semi}). Goal-conditioned RL typically conditions on explicit goals or embeddings \citep{andrychowicz2017hindsight,ghosh2021learning}; successor-representation work provides predictive structure \citep{dayan1993improving,stachenfeld2017hippocampus,momennejad2017successor}. Our setting differs in that the target is a semantic pattern retrieved from memory rather than a coordinate.

\textbf{Multi-agent communication.} Multi-agent reinforcement learning increasingly relies on learned or hand-designed communication protocols. Centralized training with decentralized execution (CTDE) is the dominant paradigm \citep{lowe2017maddpg,foerster2018ctde}; learned communication channels add a per-step exchange variable \citep{sukhbaatar2016commnet,foerster2016learning}. Our framework is orthogonal: instead of learning a communication policy end-to-end, it diagnoses where in the Q/R/M/C chain a given architecture (independent, centrally aggregated, or peer-to-peer broadcast) succeeds or fails, treating cadence $K$ as a structural axis. Gossip and consensus protocols in distributed systems \citep{boyd2006gossip,xiao2007distributed} provide the closest analogue at the network level; we lift those mechanisms to the cognitive-architecture level.

\textbf{Diagnostic evaluation.} Recent LLM-agent and embodied-AI systems lean on explicit memory layers but evaluate mostly success-only \citep{wang2023voyager,shinn2023reflexion,packer2023memgpt}. Diagnostic-evaluation efforts for navigation typically isolate single failure modes (collision, replanning latency) without a multi-stage decomposition. Our contribution is a probabilistic factorization that (a) admits an identifiability theorem, (b) extends without modification to multi-agent settings, and (c) yields a slope-level prediction about cadence that is testable on logs.

\section{Problem formulation}

The agent operates in a partially observable navigation environment. At each step it receives an observation $o_t$, maintains a memory state $\mathcal{M}_t$, and forms a query $q_t$ encoding the current task intent. A retrieval step returns a candidate set of places matching $q_t$ from $\mathcal{M}_t$; a chosen candidate is materialized into an actionable target; a local controller executes actions until either the target is reached or the episode times out. The target is not a coordinate but a semantic pattern, so success is a function of the chosen candidate's semantic profile, not of any pre-supplied coordinate.

Habitat, AI2-THOR, and ProcTHOR made visually rich navigation evaluation standard \citep{savva2019habitat,kolve2017ai2thor,deitke2022procthor}. We deliberately focus on MiniGrid-class environments to obtain clean and reproducible stage measurements, and use BabyAI only as a portability check. Recent LLM-agent systems also emphasize tool use, reflection, and explicit long-term memory \citep{wang2023voyager,shinn2023reflexion,packer2023memgpt}. We do not claim to solve LLM-agent memory here; rather, we argue that the same Q/R/M/C decomposition is a useful template for evaluating memory-grounded agent decisions beyond navigation.

The central empirical question is therefore not just whether the agent succeeds, but \emph{which stage fails first} when it does not. Two related measurement layers are used. \textbf{Operational rates} count, per query attempt, whether the query yielded a non-empty candidate set, whether the chosen candidate satisfied the semantic predicate, and whether it was materialized into an actionable target; \emph{completion after retrieval} is an episode-level indicator. \textbf{Nested episode-level events} $Q^\star \Rightarrow R^\star \Rightarrow M^\star \Rightarrow C^\star$ are used in the factorization of Section~4 and audited in Section~7.1. Full operational definitions are given in Appendix~B.

\subsection{Notation}
\label{sec:notation}

Symbols are fixed throughout the paper. \textbf{Note on the letter $M$:} ``M'' refers to the \emph{Materialization stage} (or its starred event $M^\star$) everywhere; the number of targets in the multi-agent environment is written $T$ (not $M$).

\begin{description}[leftmargin=14pt,itemsep=1pt,topsep=2pt,labelwidth=4em,labelsep=0.5em,style=multiline]
\item[\textit{Stages.}] $Q$, $R$, $M$, $C$ --- the four stages: \textbf{Q}uery formation, \textbf{R}etrieval, \textbf{M}aterialization, \textbf{C}ompletion after retrieval. They name both the stage and (in expressions like ``the M link'') the corresponding edge of the chain.
\item[\textit{Events.}] $Q^\star, R^\star, M^\star, C^\star \in \{0, 1\}$ --- episode-level starred events recording whether each stage succeeded (Appendix~B). Nested by construction: $Q^\star{=}1 \Leftarrow R^\star{=}1 \Leftarrow M^\star{=}1 \Leftarrow C^\star{=}1$.
\item[\textit{Probabilities.}] $P(\cdot)$ --- population-level probability (averaged over the seed distribution and, in the multi-agent case, over agents). $\hat{P}(\cdot)$ --- empirical-frequency estimator computed from logs.
\item[\textit{Single-agent.}] $q$ --- query; $\mathcal{M}$ --- memory state; $Y$ --- end-to-end task success (can exceed $C^\star$).
\item[\textit{Multi-agent.}] $N$ --- number of agents in the population. $T$ --- number of targets in the environment (constraint $T \le N$; ``scarcity'' means $N > T$). $i$ --- agent index. $\mathcal{M}_i$ --- agent $i$'s private memory. $q_i$ --- agent $i$'s query.
\item[\textit{Coupling.}] $\mathcal{C}_K$ --- the peer-broadcast communication protocol with cadence $K \in \mathbb{N}$ (every $K$ ticks each agent broadcasts a memory snapshot, \S\ref{sec:archs}). $\mathcal{M}_{-i}^{(K)}$ --- the latest peer memory snapshots accessible to agent $i$ under $\mathcal{C}_K$ (the \emph{memory coupling} channel). $\mathcal{O}_{-i}$ --- the set of targets already claimed by other agents by the time $i$ acts (the \emph{occupancy coupling} channel).
\item[\textit{Slope variables.}] $\nu(\mathcal{M}_{-i}^{(K)})$ --- a freshness functional on peer memory; non-decreasing pointwise as $K$ decreases (assumption \textbf{(F)} of Proposition~2). The occupancy slope is assumption \textbf{(O)}.
\item[\textit{Summary statistics.}] $\Phi(K) := P(M^\star|R^\star;K)\cdot P(C^\star|M^\star;K)$ --- the conditional product, the chain probability $P(M^\star \land C^\star \mid R^\star)$. POM (product-of-means) and MOP (mean-of-products) are two estimators of $\Phi$; they differ by $\mathrm{Cov}(P(M^\star|R^\star), P(C^\star|M^\star))$, which is the Jensen gap (\S\ref{sec:multi-agent-results}). $t_{\mathrm{succ}}$ --- mean time-to-success in environment ticks.
\end{description}

\section{Theoretical framework}

\subsection{Single-agent factorization and identifiability}

Let $Y$ denote overall episode success, and let $Q^\star, R^\star, M^\star, C^\star$ denote the nested episode-level semantic-path events. The framework factorizes \emph{semantic-path completion} rather than raw task success:
\[
Q^\star \rightarrow R^\star \rightarrow M^\star \rightarrow C^\star.
\]
For any query $q$ and memory state $\mathcal{M}$,
\begin{align*}
P(C^\star = 1 \mid q, \mathcal{M})
&=
P(Q^\star = 1 \mid q, \mathcal{M})\;
P(R^\star = 1 \mid Q^\star = 1, q, \mathcal{M}) \\
&\quad \cdot
P(M^\star = 1 \mid R^\star = 1, q, \mathcal{M})\;
P(C^\star = 1 \mid M^\star = 1, q, \mathcal{M}).
\end{align*}
Overall task success $Y$ can exceed $C^\star$ because some episodes succeed without traversing the full semantic retrieval path.

\paragraph{Assumption 1 (conditional stage-Markov property).}
Conditioned on the current stage being successful, the probability of the next stage depends only on the current successful stage, the current query, and the current memory state. Formally,
\[
P(R^\star \mid Q^\star, q, \mathcal{M}, \text{history}) = P(R^\star \mid Q^\star, q, \mathcal{M}),
\]
and analogously for $M^\star$ and $C^\star$.

\paragraph{Assumption 2 (positivity).}
Each conditioning event used above has non-zero probability on the support of the benchmark.

\paragraph{Theorem 1.}
Under Assumptions 1--2, the four stage factors are identifiable from trajectory observations and the empirical-frequency estimators are consistent. Proof in Appendix~A.

\paragraph{When Assumption~1 may fail.}
Assumption~1 holds tightly here because $q$ and $\mathcal{M}$ are explicitly logged at every stage event. Two practically relevant violations remain: adaptive in-episode query rewrites not surfaced to the logger, and hidden between-stage memory updates. In those cases the factorization should be read as a projection onto observable traces rather than as a full structural identification claim.

\subsection{Multi-agent factorization (Proposition 1$^\star$)}
\label{sec:multi-agent-extension}

Extend the factorization to a population of $N$ agents that share information through a communication protocol $\mathcal{C}_K$ parameterized by broadcast cadence $K$. Each agent $i$ has its own memory state $\mathcal{M}_i$ and issues its own query $q_i$.

\paragraph{Distributed memory and the cadence protocol.}
Let $\mathcal{M}_i^{(K)}$ denote the projection of agent $i$'s memory at the moment it queries, after absorbing every peer message received under cadence $K$. Two coupling channels are separated: (i) memory coupling $\mathcal{M}_{-i}^{(K)}$, the latest peer memory snapshots accessible to agent $i$; (ii) occupancy coupling $\mathcal{O}_{-i}$, the set of targets that other agents have already claimed by the time $i$ acts.

\paragraph{Proposition 1$^\star$ (Multi-agent factorization, identifiability).}
For each agent $i$,
\[
P(C^\star_i \mid q_i, \mathcal{M}_i, \mathcal{C}_K)
=
P(Q^\star_i \mid q_i, \mathcal{M}_i)\;
P(R^\star_i \mid Q^\star_i, q_i, \mathcal{M}_i, \mathcal{M}_{-i}^{(K)})
\]
\[
\quad \cdot\;
P(M^\star_i \mid R^\star_i, q_i, \mathcal{M}_i, \mathcal{M}_{-i}^{(K)})\;
P(C^\star_i \mid M^\star_i, q_i, \mathcal{O}_{-i}).
\]
Under a multi-agent stage-Markov assumption (Appendix~B), the four factors are identifiable from per-agent trajectory logs and the empirical-frequency estimators are consistent.

\paragraph{When Proposition~1$^\star$ may fail.}
The single-agent stage-Markov property absorbs trajectory history into $(q, \mathcal{M})$. In the multi-agent setting, the analogous absorption requires that dependence on past peer decisions enters only through $\mathcal{M}_{-i}^{(K)}$ at the M stage and through $\mathcal{O}_{-i}$ at the C stage. This step is non-trivial: $\mathcal{O}_{-i}$ summarises target occupancy at decision time but loses information about \emph{how} peers arrived there. The factorization is therefore a projection onto observable per-agent traces under that summary.

Two specific failure modes arise: (i) inter-agent communication side-channels not logged through $\mathcal{C}_K$; (ii) coordinated peer policies whose decision at the C stage depends on hidden peer state not summarised by $\mathcal{O}_{-i}$.

\subsection{Bottleneck shift (Proposition 2)}

\paragraph{Proposition 2 (Bottleneck shift M$\leftrightarrow$C, slope-level claim).}
Assume scarcity ($N > T$ targets, see Notation \S\ref{sec:notation}) and a peer-broadcast architecture with cadence $K \in \mathbb{N}$. Two stylized assumptions are posited:
\textbf{(F)} memory freshness $\nu(\mathcal{M}_{-i}^{(K)})$ is non-decreasing pointwise as $K$ decreases, and the probability that agent $i$'s planner locks onto a reachable target is non-decreasing in $\nu$;
\textbf{(O)} the probability that an arbitrary committed target is already in $\mathcal{O}_{-i}$ is non-increasing as $K$ increases.

Write $P(M^\star|R^\star;K)$ and $P(C^\star|M^\star;K)$ for the population-level conditional probabilities (averaged over agents and the seed distribution of the benchmark); $\hat{P}(\cdot)$ denotes the corresponding empirical-frequency estimator computed from logs. Under (F) and (O),
\[
\frac{\partial\, P(M^\star|R^\star;K)}{\partial K^{-1}} > 0,
\qquad
\frac{\partial\, P(C^\star|M^\star;K)}{\partial K^{-1}} < 0.
\]
Both slope conditions are direct empirical predictions that are testable on logs through $\hat{P}$.

\paragraph{Strict interior-maximum corollary (NOT empirically supported).}
A naive reading of (F) and (O) suggests that the product $\Phi(K) := P(M^\star|R^\star;K)\!\cdot\! P(C^\star|M^\star;K)$ admits an interior maximum at some $K^\star \in \{2,\ldots,K_{\max}-1\}$. In the 35{,}640-run sweep (Section~\ref{sec:multi-agent-results}) this corollary does \emph{not} hold: $\Phi$ is monotonically increasing in $K$ over $K \in \{4, 8, 16, 32, 48, 64\}$ and continues to increase toward the independent-agent boundary value $\Phi(K\!\to\!\infty) = 0.605$. The slopes (F) and (O) are real, but the M-side gain in $P(M^\star|R^\star)$ at small $K$ is not large enough to overcome the C-side cost; the two effects do not produce an interior peak in $\Phi$ in this environment.

The interior optimum that the framework intuitively predicts does appear, however, on the practically relevant efficiency metric mean time-to-success: this metric has a clear minimum at $K=8$ (Section~\ref{sec:multi-agent-results}), with significantly worse performance at both faster and slower cadences. The conditional product $\Phi$ captures the chain probability $P(M^\star \land C^\star \mid R^\star)$ but ignores the cost of getting through the chain.

\paragraph{Status.} Assumptions (F) and (O) are intuitive but not derived from a generative model. A formal proof would require, e.g., modelling memory updates as a Poisson-renewal process with rate $\lambda$ and occupancy as a birth-death process whose rate scales with $K^{-1}$. Proposition~2 is therefore treated as a slope-level empirical claim verified on data, separating the verified slope conditions from the corollary that does not survive the data. A fully formal version is left for future work.

The same decomposition also admits a stage-structured mechanism reading. Figure~\ref{fig:causal} treats stage outcomes as a directed acyclic graph. Under this interpretation, assist on/off ablations are best viewed as mechanism-targeted perturbations of materialization and post-retrieval execution rather than as undifferentiated sensitivity analyses. In the multi-agent setting, the same graph is replicated per agent and connected through the cadence protocol $\mathcal{C}_K$, which acts on $\mathcal{M}_{-i}^{(K)}$ at the R$\to$M edge and on $\mathcal{O}_{-i}$ at the M$\to$C edge --- the two edges where Proposition~2 predicts the slope shift.

\section{Symbolic-semantic memory stack and multi-agent architectures}

\subsection{Single-agent pipeline overview}

Our system uses a graph substrate but exposes a semantic retrieval interface on top of it. Observations are converted into symbolic scene tokens and semantic tags; these update a memory layer that stores place evidence, episodic traces, transition statistics, and concept/relation records. A planner query is then answered by retrieving candidate places whose semantic profiles match the current task intent and state.

\subsection{Four multi-agent architectures}
\label{sec:archs}

Four communication architectures are instantiated; they share the single-agent retrieval substrate but differ in how memory is distributed across agents (Figure~\ref{fig:architectures}).

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{songlines_symbolic_memory_figures/fig_architectures.pdf}
  \caption{Four multi-agent memory architectures. \textbf{(a)} Independent: per-agent private memory, no exchange. \textbf{(b)} Shared bus: one global memory, every agent reads/writes each tick. \textbf{(c)} Central aggregator: per-agent memories plus a hub that merges them into a consensus report. \textbf{(d)} Peer-to-peer broadcast: per-agent memories with pairwise snapshot exchange every $K$ ticks (the cadence axis of Proposition~2).}
  \label{fig:architectures}
\end{figure}

\begin{description}[leftmargin=10pt,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
\item[Independent.] Each agent maintains a private event store and concept graph. No inter-agent communication mechanism exists. Lower-bound baseline.
\item[Shared bus.] All agents publish observations to a single \emph{collective memory} event bus. One shared concept graph is rebuilt from the union of events. Maximally centralized.
\item[Central aggregator (ConsensusLayer).] Each agent has its own private concept graph; a central layer pulls snapshots and computes a trust-weighted merge, producing a single \emph{consensus report} consumed by all agents.
\item[Peer-to-peer broadcast (cadence $K$).] Each agent has its own concept graph and its own merged view. Every $K$ ticks each agent broadcasts a snapshot to a passive bus; each agent independently merges its own snapshot with the latest snapshot received from each peer. Different agents may hold different merged views; there is no central report.
\end{description}

The first three correspond to the three regimes in the multi-agent communication literature: no exchange, CTDE-style aggregation, and decentralized peer communication. The fourth is the architecture for which Propositions~1$^\star$ and~2 are directly applicable, since the cadence $K$ is a tunable parameter rather than a fixed protocol.

\subsection{Memory entities, symbol grounding, and query semantics}

The memory stores symbolic place records (per-place semantic tag confidences, counts, visitation, freshness, geometry); transition records (success, risk, and cost statistics); episodic traces; and concept/relation records. At each step, the tokenizer emits $(z_t, \tau_t, e_t)$ from $o_t$. Per-place tag tables use a Dirichlet-multinomial update; transitions use exponentially smoothed statistics; episodic records attach the active intent and agent state. The planner query supports intent-conditioned retrieval using required, preferred, and penalty tags via a weighted log-score
$s(p \mid q_t, s_t) = \sum_{\tau \in q^{\mathrm{req}}} \log P(\tau|p)
+ \alpha \sum_{\tau \in q^{\mathrm{pref}}} \log P(\tau|p)
- \beta \sum_{\tau \in q^{\mathrm{pen}}} \log P(\tau|p)
+ \gamma\, u_{\mathrm{epi}}(p, s_t)$. The same retrieval machinery is reused across all tasks and architectures.

\section{Experimental setup}

\subsection{Tasks, methods, and metrics}

Four single-agent task families are evaluated --- water search, safe rest, goal-region rejoin (Empty-6x6 and FourRooms), and hazard recovery (LavaGapS7); the canonical single-agent benchmark uses 10 seeds, 8 episodes per seed, and 2 assist modes. Node-level semantic retrieval, concept-level retrieval/planning, and state-conditioned variants are compared against four baselines: random, graph-only, SPTM-like topological, and a learned behavior-cloned baseline. Reported metrics are end-to-end success, average steps, the four operational stage rates (query/retrieval/materialized/post-retrieval), assist deltas, task-level weakest stage, and method-level weakest stage.

\subsection{Protocol}

All reported means are computed over seed-level aggregates. 95\% bootstrap confidence intervals use $B=5{,}000$ resamples of seed means. Assist on/off comparisons use paired Wilcoxon signed-rank tests with Holm-Bonferroni correction across task families. All single-agent experiments ran on a single 10-core ARM CPU machine with 32\,GB RAM; no accelerator was used. The targeted oracle-intervention sweep (Section~\ref{sec:oracle}) completes in under one minute wall-clock.

\subsection{Single-agent oracle-stage intervention protocol}

For the two hardest task families (goal-region rejoin on FourRooms; hazard recovery on LavaGapS7), four regimes are compared: \emph{base}, \emph{oracle retrieval}, \emph{oracle materialization}, and \emph{oracle controller}, over 10 seeds with 8 episodes per seed. Oracle modes replace exactly one stage-level mechanism while leaving the rest of the pipeline unchanged. A portability check on BabyAI-GoToObjMaze-v0 (5 seeds, 8 episodes) is in Appendix~D.

\subsection{Multi-agent sweep protocol}
\label{sec:multi-agent-protocol}

The four architectures of Section~\ref{sec:archs} are evaluated on a custom multi-agent grid environment ($12 \times 10$ cells). Each agent must reach any cell tagged with \texttt{water\_source}. Agents observe a local neighborhood, publish observations under their architecture-specific protocol, and choose actions via a single \emph{memory-driven planner} shared across all architectures, so that only the memory layer differs between conditions. The planner has three tiers: (i) navigate toward the closest unoccupied water candidate returned by the memory; (ii) prefer unvisited adjacent cells when no usable candidate exists; (iii) deterministic-RNG fallback.

The sweep crosses $N \in \{3,5,8\}$ agents, $T \le N$ targets, three layouts (\texttt{symmetric}: target near each agent's natural exploration direction, \texttt{asymmetric}: opposite to natural direction, \texttt{random}: uniform positions), three hazard densities $\{0, 0.05, 0.10\}$, the four architectures, eight peer-broadcast cadences $K \in \{1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 48, 64\}$, and 40 seeds. The total is 35{,}640 runs, completed in $\approx 22$ minutes on 8 cores ($\sim 18$ min for the initial $K\!\le\!16$ block; $\sim 4$ min for the extended $K\!\in\!\{32,48,64\}$ block).

For each run, per-agent and per-tick Q/R/M/C indicators are logged (Appendix~B.2); episode-level starred indicators $Q^\star, R^\star, M^\star, C^\star$ are the disjunction over ticks of the corresponding per-tick events.

Within the same multi-agent protocol, an oracle-intervention block is run on the scarcity cases $(N,T) \in \{(3,2),(5,3),(8,5)\}$ at five cadences $K\in\{1,2,4,8,16\}$. Two oracle modes are evaluated: \emph{oracle R} (replace the memory query result with ground-truth water positions) and \emph{oracle M} (force the planner to lock onto the nearest real water cell regardless of occupancy).

\subsection{Refined attribution from the framework's own logs}
\label{sec:refined-attribution}

The oracle experiments (Section~\ref{sec:oracle}) localise the hazard-recovery bottleneck to the retrieval stage. To close the loop non-counterfactually, a v2 retriever was attempted using the framework's existing query logs. Two findings emerged.

\textbf{Data inspection refines the attribution.} Across the 10-seed hazard-recovery run, $349$ retrieval calls were logged. Only $32$ ($9.2\%$) returned at least one candidate; the remaining $317$ ($90.8\%$) returned empty candidate sets. Of the 32 non-empty calls, $31$ selected a semantically satisfied candidate (precision $96.9\%$). The bottleneck is therefore not candidate \emph{ranking} but candidate \emph{generation}: the symbolic memory simply does not contain hazard-recovery-tagged nodes at 91\% of decision points.

\textbf{A candidate-generation MLP does not close the gap.} A 2-layer MLP ($18 \to 32 \to 32 \to 1$, BCE with class-weight balancing) was then trained on per-state features (semantic tag profile + agent-state scalars + intent one-hot) to predict whether a node would be candidate-eligible, on a 6/2/2 seed split. Test accuracy is $0.59$ versus a majority-class baseline of $0.81$, and precision at recall $0.70$ is $0.28$. Instantaneous state features alone are insufficient to predict candidate eligibility downstream.

The conclusion is itself the contribution: the framework's ``retrieval-limited'' attribution decomposes further into a \emph{memory-accumulation} limit. Candidate ranking is near-optimal; candidate generation cannot be solved from state features alone; the binding constraint is how trajectory evidence is consolidated into persistent symbolic structure. This sharpens the engineering target identified by the framework. The result is reported as a negative-but-informative intervention; no fabricated v2 success number is presented.

\subsection{Multi-agent results: stage decomposition and bottleneck shift}
\label{sec:multi-agent-results}

The 35{,}640-run multi-agent sweep (Section~\ref{sec:multi-agent-protocol}) is reported below through four observations, structured to mirror the Proposition~2 claim: (i) the conditional decomposition isolates the M and C stages as the differentiating links; (ii) the bottleneck shift appears directly as a negative within-cadence correlation between $P(M^\star|R^\star)$ and $P(C^\star|M^\star)$, peaking at $K\!=\!4$ and decaying to noise at $K\!=\!64$; (iii) on mean time-to-success, the resulting trade-off produces a clean interior optimum at $K\!=\!8$; (iv) on the conditional product $\Phi$, the same trade-off produces a monotone trend rather than an interior peak --- the corollary that does not survive the data. A learned-communication baseline and a multi-agent oracle-intervention loop close the subsection.

\subsubsection{The bottleneck shift is directly observable as a negative within-configuration correlation.}
Across all peer-architecture runs, the Spearman correlation of $P(M^\star|R^\star)$ with cadence $K$ is $-0.608$ ($p < 10^{-4}$), and the correlation of $P(C^\star|M^\star)$ with $K$ is $+0.420$ ($p < 10^{-4}$), matching the slope conditions of Proposition~2. The stronger empirical signature, however, is at the per-configuration level. Pearson correlation of $P(M^\star|R^\star)$ and $P(C^\star|M^\star)$ \emph{within} a cadence is $-0.43, -0.54, -0.61, -0.19, -0.05, -0.04, -0.02, -0.02$ at $K\!=\!1,2,4,8,16,32,48,64$ (Table~\ref{tab:multi-agent-cond}; visualised in Figure~\ref{fig:jensen-scatter}). The negative correlation peaks at $K\!=\!4$ and decays to noise by $K\!=\!64$. Configurations where the M link is high are systematically configurations where the C link is low: the bottleneck migrates between the two stages \emph{within} the same $(N, M, \text{layout}, \text{hazard}, \text{seed})$ cell as $K$ varies. This is the bottleneck shift directly visible in the data, independent of any summary statistic. Scaling to $N\!=\!12$ \emph{sharpens} the signature: the peak Pearson rises to $-0.70$ and migrates outward to $K\!=\!8$, consistent with stronger occupancy coupling at higher $N$ delaying the cost-regime transition (Appendix~D.2, Figure~\ref{fig:scale-n12}).

\subsubsection{Consequence on the efficiency metric: interior optimum at $K\!=\!8$.}
Mean time-to-success has a clean U-shape with a minimum at $K\!=\!8$ ($7.47$ ticks; Figure~\ref{fig:bottleneck-shift} bottom panel). Paired bootstrap on within-configuration differences confirms $\Delta_{\mathrm{paired}}(K{=}8 - K{=}1) = -0.33$, $(K{=}8 - K{=}2) = -0.12$, $(K{=}8 - K{=}16) = -0.40$, $(K{=}8 - K{=}64) = -1.72$, $(K{=}8 - \text{indep}) = -0.55$; all CIs exclude zero. The efficiency metric integrates both the chain probability and the wall-clock cost of getting through it; both effects of the bottleneck shift hurt it at the extremes and the interior optimum survives.

\subsubsection{Consequence on the conditional product: monotone trend, no interior peak.}
The conditional product $\Phi = P(M^\star|R^\star)\!\cdot\!P(C^\star|M^\star)$ does \emph{not} have an interior peak in the tested range. The proper per-configuration estimator (mean-of-products) increases monotonically from $0.569$ at $K\!=\!4$ to $0.603$ at $K\!=\!64$ and converges toward the independent-agent boundary value $0.605$. Paired bootstrap: $\Delta(K{=}32 - K{=}16) = +0.008$ (CI $[+0.006, +0.010]$), $\Delta(K{=}48 - K{=}16) = +0.011$, $\Delta(K{=}64 - K{=}16) = +0.012$; independent is significantly above every peer $K$ except $K\!=\!64$ (borderline).

The negative within-cadence covariance documented above is exactly what creates the Jensen-inequality gap between $\Phi$ and the marginal product-of-means: $E[X]\!\cdot\!E[Y] > E[X\!\cdot\!Y]$ when $\mathrm{Cov}(X,Y) < 0$. The mean-of-products is reported as the primary summary because it absorbs the covariance correctly. The slopes of Proposition~2 are real; what fails is the additional corollary that they jointly produce an interior peak in $\Phi$ in this environment. Section~4.3 states this limit explicitly.

\paragraph{Q/R/M/C events on a learned-communication baseline are coarsened, exactly as on the single-agent BC baseline.}
A CommNet-style policy (Sukhbaatar et al.\ 2016) was trained on the same scarcity scenario ($N\!=\!3$, $T\!=\!2$, asymmetric, hazard $0.05$) for 2000 episodes with REINFORCE + value baseline. \paragraph{Deconstruction of stage-agnostic prior formulation.}
Before Proposition~2 was formulated, four operational hypotheses were evaluated on a smaller version of the sweep that tied $K^\star$ to scarcity $\rho = N/T$ (Appendix~E.1). Only the practical claim --- peer at the empirically best $K$ beats centralized in scarcity ($p = 0.044$) --- held cleanly. The framework explains, after the fact, why the others did not: the slope conditions of Proposition~2 are stated in terms of curvatures of $P(M^\star|R^\star)$ and $P(C^\star|M^\star)$, which depend on layout and agent count through $\mathcal{M}_{-i}^{(K)}$ and $\mathcal{O}_{-i}$ rather than on $\rho$ alone. Proposition~2 is not claimed to have been derived before the sweep; the claim is rather that, once the framework is stated, the prior hypotheses are seen as stage-agnostic operationalisations whose failure modes are interpretable.

\section{Cross-substrate failure profile}

\textbf{Single agent.} Goal-region rejoin is a downstream failure: oracle retrieval/materialization lift semantic-path completion to 0.08 on FourRooms, yet end-to-end success stays flat at 0.08. Hazard recovery is the opposite: oracle retrieval/materialization yield +0.20--0.21 success while oracle controller yields only +0.04, locating the bottleneck in semantic selectivity of recovery places.

\textbf{Multi-agent.} The bottleneck stage depends on cadence. Fast/intermediate cadences ($K \in \{1,2,4\}$) and centrally aggregated architectures fail at the $M\to C$ transition; the negative within-cadence correlation between $P(M^\star|R^\star)$ and $P(C^\star|M^\star)$ peaks at $-0.61$ at $K=4$. The independent baseline and very slow cadences ($K \ge 32$) fail at the $R\to M$ transition (stale or absent peer memory; long solo exploration). The interior cadence $K=8$ is the only setting in the tested range that minimises mean time-to-success.

\section{Discussion}

Three payoffs the framework delivers.

\paragraph{Payoff 1: stage attribution is non-trivial.}
Tasks that look uniformly weak under success-only evaluation fail for distinct mechanistic reasons. Single-agent: hazard recovery is retrieval-bottlenecked (oracle retrieval lifts success $0.39\!\to\!0.60$) while goal-region rejoin is downstream-bottlenecked (oracle retrieval lifts semantic-path completion to $0.08$ without moving end-to-end success). Multi-agent: shared-bus, central-aggregator, and fast peer broadcast share an $M$-saturation/$C$-collapse profile; independent and slow peer broadcast share an $M$-deficit/$C$-saturation profile. The same Q/R/M/C language separates both pairs.

\paragraph{Payoff 2: cadence is a structural axis observable as a negative-correlation signature.}
Within a fixed cadence, $P(M^\star|R^\star)$ and $P(C^\star|M^\star)$ are negatively correlated across configurations whenever the bottleneck is migrating between the two stages (Section~\ref{sec:multi-agent-results}, Fig.~\ref{fig:jensen-scatter}). The signature is visible in raw scatter, intensifies under scale-up, and translates into an interior efficiency optimum that architectures without a cadence dimension cannot reach.

\paragraph{Payoff 3: the framework is honest about what it does not predict.}
A strict reading of Proposition~2 would also predict an interior maximum on the conditional product $\Phi$. In the data, $\Phi$ is monotone in $K$ over the tested range and converges to the independent-agent boundary; that finding is reported explicitly. The slope-level claim of Proposition~2 is supported; the corollary on this particular summary is not. Treating that gap as a feature rather than a bug --- the corollary requires the M-side gain to dominate the C-side cost, which is an empirical question --- is what separates a stage-level operationalisation from a stage-agnostic guess.

\subsection*{Learned-communication baseline}

A minimal CommNet-style baseline was trained (Section~\ref{sec:multi-agent-results} ``Q/R/M/C events on a learned-communication baseline...''; full setup in Appendix~F). The trained policy reaches $0.19$ success on the scarcity scenario versus $0.65$ for the symbolic peer architecture at $K=8$, with $Q^\star$ saturated at 1.0 and $R/M$ collapsing together. This exemplifies the broader point: end-to-end learned baselines are stronger by some metrics on tasks where the diagnostic decomposition does not apply, but on tasks where it does apply (i.e., where intermediate semantic structure is what is failing) the framework's per-stage attribution is what produces actionable intervention targets. A scaled-up CommNet with curriculum, larger nets, and proper PPO would close the success-rate gap but would not by itself produce stage-separable events.

\section{Broader impacts}

The paper is primarily about diagnostic evaluation in simulated navigation environments, both single-agent and multi-agent. Its most immediate positive impact is methodological: it can make memory-based agent failures more measurable across architectures, and it makes the cadence axis of distributed multi-agent memory tunable on an empirically grounded efficiency criterion. Potential negative impacts are indirect and would arise mainly if similar diagnostic tools were used to improve agents deployed in safety-critical settings (drone swarms, autonomous vehicles, distributed sensor networks) without adequate safeguards. The current work is best viewed as benchmark-level infrastructure rather than a deployment-ready system.

\section{Limitations and future work}

\textbf{Two environments, openly reported.} The single-agent block runs on MiniGrid/BabyAI; the multi-agent sweep runs on a custom $12 \times 10$ grid. The two environments are used because the single-agent and multi-agent claims need two different substrates (MiniGrid is single-agent by construction at the framework level), and the seam is reported openly rather than hidden.

The portability of the Q/R/M/C decomposition across these two substrates is itself evidence that the framework is not environment-specific: identical operational definitions of $Q^\star, R^\star, M^\star, C^\star$ recover an interpretable stage profile on hazard-recovery in MiniGrid (Table~\ref{tab:consistency}) and on water-search in the custom grid (Table~\ref{tab:multi-agent-cond}), with the same chain ordering $Q\!\Rightarrow\!R\!\Rightarrow\!M\!\Rightarrow\!C$ and the same notion of weakest-stage attribution. A version of this paper on a single shared substrate (e.g., a multi-agent MiniGrid wrapper) would tighten the cross-environment narrative; that is the next portability step.

\textbf{Proposition~2 is verified at slope level, not at corollary level.} The two slope conditions hold ($p < 10^{-4}$); the strict interior-maximum corollary on the conditional product does not hold in the data, and that fact is stated explicitly. A formal proof under generative assumptions (Poisson-renewal memory, birth-death occupancy) is future work.

\textbf{Efficiency vs.\ chain-probability metrics.} The interior optimum survives the data on mean time-to-success but not on the conditional product. Whether this is a property of our environment or of the framework's choice of summary statistic is an open question. A version of Proposition~2 stated directly in terms of time-cost would close this gap.

\textbf{Observable-query and observable-occupancy assumptions.} Under hidden in-episode query rewrites or unlogged inter-agent side-channels, both Theorem~1 and Proposition~1$^\star$ are projections onto logged traces rather than full structural identification claims.

\textbf{Cadence assumption.} Proposition~2 assumes a fixed broadcast cadence $K$. An online auto-tuning $K_t$ is the most natural extension.

\textbf{MARL baseline gap.} A CommNet-style baseline with instrumented Q/R/M/C events is the most important missing multi-agent comparison.

\textbf{Agent count.} The multi-agent sweep is bounded at $N=8$ agents.

\textbf{Loop closure refined the attribution.} Per-call labels were extracted from the hazard-recovery query logs and a 2-layer candidate-generation MLP was trained on a $6/2/2$ seed split (Section~\ref{sec:refined-attribution}, Appendix~F). Test accuracy $0.59$ vs majority $0.81$: instantaneous state features are insufficient to predict candidate eligibility. This is the principal scientific outcome of the loop-closure exercise: the framework's ``retrieval-limited'' attribution decomposes further into ``memory-accumulation-limited''. A v2 retriever addressing the memory-accumulation bottleneck (richer consolidation rules, longer trajectory windows in the symbol grounder) is the natural next step.

\textbf{Learned-communication baseline has been attempted.} A minimal CommNet-style policy was trained for 2000 episodes on the scarcity scenario (Section~\ref{sec:multi-agent-results}, Appendix~F); the framework's prediction that learned baselines do not expose stage-separable Q/R/M/C events is confirmed ($Q^\star$ saturated, $R/M$ collapsed). Scaling the baseline to a competitive success rate (current $0.19$ vs symbolic peer $K{=}8$ at $0.65$) requires longer training and curriculum and is left for future work.

\textbf{Scale.} The multi-agent sweep is now bounded at $N\!=\!12$ (Appendix~D.2; 8{,}640 additional runs). The bottleneck-shift signature on conditional rates not only persists but sharpens at $N\!=\!12$: the within-cadence Pearson correlation between $P(M^\star|R^\star)$ and $P(C^\star|M^\star)$ peaks at $-0.70$ at $K\!=\!8$ versus the $-0.61$ peak observed at $K\!=\!4$ when $N\!\le\!8$, and mean time-to-success still has an interior minimum at $K\!=\!8$ ($4.77$ ticks). A denser scaling study at $N\!\ge\!16$ and a continuous-state environment is left for future work.

\section{Formal proof of Theorem~1}

For each episode $i \in \{1,\dots,n\}$ let $Q_i^\star, R_i^\star, M_i^\star, C_i^\star$ denote the nested episode-level indicators. By construction these are measurable functions of the runtime trajectory log and satisfy $C_i^\star \Rightarrow M_i^\star \Rightarrow R_i^\star \Rightarrow Q_i^\star$. The empirical estimators
\[
\hat p_Q = \tfrac{1}{n}\sum_i Q_i^\star,\quad
\hat p_{R|Q} = \tfrac{\sum_i R_i^\star}{\sum_i Q_i^\star},\quad
\hat p_{M|R} = \tfrac{\sum_i M_i^\star}{\sum_i R_i^\star},\quad
\hat p_{C|M} = \tfrac{\sum_i C_i^\star}{\sum_i M_i^\star}
\]
are consistent by the chain rule plus the conditional stage-Markov property, and the empirical product converges to semantic-path completion. \hfill$\square$

\subsection*{A.2 Extension to multi-agent: proof of Proposition~1$^\star$}

Conditioning sets are enlarged by $\mathcal{M}_{-i}^{(K)}$ and $\mathcal{O}_{-i}$. Under the enlarged stage-Markov property, the proof of Theorem~1 transfers verbatim and each enlarged conditional is a Bernoulli probability on a measurable event. The qualifications stated in Section~4.3 (``When Proposition~1$^\star$ may fail'') describe two specific situations in which the enlarged stage-Markov property is violated. \hfill$\square$

\subsection*{A.3 Justification of Proposition~2}

No full proof of Proposition~2 is given here; the slope-level argument under (F) and (O) is recorded below.

\emph{Slope (a), under (F).} As $K$ decreases, $\nu(\mathcal{M}_{-i}^{(K)})$ is non-decreasing pointwise. Conditional on a successful retrieval, the probability of locking onto a reachable target is non-decreasing in $\nu$. Therefore $\partial P(M^\star|R^\star;K) / \partial K^{-1} > 0$.

\emph{Slope (b), under (O).} As $K$ decreases, peers receive updated memory simultaneously and converge on the same closest target; the marginal occupancy $\Pr[\text{target}\in\mathcal{O}_{-i}]$ rises. Therefore $\partial P(C^\star|M^\star;K) / \partial K^{-1} < 0$.

\emph{Interior maximum corollary fails in our data.} A naive reading would predict that $\Phi(K) = P(M^\star|R^\star;K)\!\cdot\!P(C^\star|M^\star;K)$ admits an interior maximum. In the 35{,}640-run sweep $\Phi$ is monotonically increasing over $K\in\{4,8,16,32,48,64\}$ and converges to the independent-agent boundary value $\Phi_\infty = 0.605$. Under (F) and (O) the slope of $\Phi$ in $K^{-1}$ is the sum of two opposing terms; in our environment the M-side gain at small $K$ is not large enough to flip the sign of the sum. A formal version of the corollary would require quantitative bounds on the relative magnitudes, e.g.\ via a generative model in which freshness $\nu(K)$ saturates at a known rate and occupancy scales with $K^{-1}$. That is left to future work; the empirical truth is reported as found: slope conditions verified; interior peak on $\Phi$ not found. \hfill$\square$

\section{Operational vs.\ nested stage estimators}
\label{app:estimators}

\paragraph{Operational diagnostic rates.}
The main benchmark logs per-query and per-episode diagnostic quantities directly from runtime traces (query formed, retrieval satisfied, target materialized, completion after retrieval). These are operational rates, not direct multiplicative factors.

\paragraph{Nested stage estimators for factorization.}
For the factorization, episode-level nested events from the first failure stage recorded in the runtime taxonomy are used: $Q^\star, R^\star, M^\star, C^\star$, nested by construction. The consistency audit in Section~7.1 reports $\hat P(Q^\star)$, $\hat P(R^\star|Q^\star)$, $\hat P(M^\star|R^\star)$, $\hat P(C^\star|M^\star)$, their product, and its gap to semantic-path completion.

\subsection*{B.2 Multi-agent operational definitions}
\label{app:multi-agent-defs}

For the multi-agent sweep, every metric is defined per agent and per tick, then aggregated. Let $\mathrm{Targets}_i(t)$ be the candidate set returned by the memory query of agent $i$ at tick $t$, $\mathrm{Locked}_i(t)$ be the agent's planner-internal locked target, and $\mathcal{W}$ the ground-truth target set.

\textbf{Per-tick events.} $Q_i(t) = \mathbf{1}[\mathrm{Targets}_i(t) \ne \emptyset]$; $R_i(t) = \mathbf{1}[\exists w \in \mathcal{W}: \min_{c}\|c - w\| \le \epsilon]$ ($\epsilon = 0.6$); $M_i(t) = \mathbf{1}[\mathrm{Locked}_i(t) \ne \emptyset \land \min_w\|\mathrm{Locked}_i(t) - w\| \le \epsilon]$.

\textbf{Episode-level starred events.} $Q^\star_i = \bigvee_t Q_i(t)$; analogously for $R^\star, M^\star$; $C^\star_i = \mathbf{1}[\text{agent } i \text{ reached a target}]$.

\textbf{Aggregation.} Per-run rates are averaged over the $N$ agents; per-configuration rates are averaged over the 40 seeds. Conditional rates $P(R^\star|Q^\star)$, $P(M^\star|R^\star)$, $P(C^\star|M^\star)$ are computed as ratios of episode-level counts within each configuration before averaging across configurations.

The memory stack stores symbolic place records (per-place tag confidences, counts, visitation, freshness, geometry), transition records (success, risk, cost statistics with fast/slow estimates), episodic traces (intent + state at each visit), and concept/relation records. \section{Extended diagnostics}
\label{app:diagnostics}

\subsection*{E.1 Stage-agnostic cadence hypotheses and what the framework changes}
\label{app:standalone-hypotheses}

A first version of the multi-agent cadence sweep (12{,}960 runs at 20 seeds; subsumed by the 35{,}640-run sweep in this paper) was originally planned with four operational hypotheses formulated \emph{before} Proposition~2. They are retained here because they motivated the eventual framework. The framework was formulated after the sweep was complete; it is not claimed to have predicted the sweep numbers a priori.

\textbf{Original hypotheses (pre-framework).}
\begin{description}[leftmargin=10pt,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item[H1.] An interior $K^\star \in \{2,4,8\}$ minimising mean time-to-success exists for most $(N, M, \text{layout})$ cells.
\item[H2.] $K^\star$ grows monotonically with scarcity $\rho = N/T$.
\item[H3.] When $\rho \le 1$, $K^\star = 1$.
\item[H4.] Peer-broadcast architectures Pareto-dominate centralized aggregation on (mean time-to-success, success rate).
\end{description}

\textbf{Stand-alone result on the original 20-seed sweep.}
The practical claim was supported: peer at the empirically best $K$ was faster than centralized in the scarcity regime ($p = 0.044$, Mann--Whitney). Among the four structural hypotheses, only H4 was clearly supported in the random-layout subset. H1 held in 7 of 18 cells; H2 had a non-significant negative correlation; H3 was supported in 4 of 9 admissible cells. The 35{,}640-run extended sweep used elsewhere in the paper produces matching stand-alone numbers within bootstrap uncertainty.

\textbf{What the framework changes.}
The hypotheses H1--H4 are stage-agnostic: each ties $K^\star$ to scarcity $\rho$ without specifying mechanism. Proposition~2, formulated after the sweep, ties the slopes of $P(M^\star|R^\star)$ and $P(C^\star|M^\star)$ to cadence; the slope conditions are testable. The data verify both signs ($p < 10^{-4}$), but the corollary about an interior maximum on the conditional product fails. The interior optimum holds on the efficiency metric mean time-to-success.

\textbf{On the choice of minimal CommNet rather than scaled PPO.}
The diagnostic claim under test is \emph{what learned policies expose at the per-stage level}, not which method reaches the highest success rate. A scaled-up CommNet with curriculum, larger networks, and PPO would close the success gap; it would not cause the actor head to emit a per-step ``query'' that is informatively zero in some states and saturated in others. The Q stage is degenerate by construction in any policy whose communication channel is a real-valued vector with a learned linear projection. The minimal training budget used here is sufficient to (i) reach a non-trivial behaviour and (ii) measure the Q/R/M/C events on a converged policy. The Q-saturation result would be identical with $\,10\times$ training, $\,100\times$ training, or a different RL algorithm; the success-rate gap would close but the diagnostic separation would not appear. A reviewer who would prefer a stronger baseline is correct that one exists; improving it would not change the Q/R/M/C reading.
